Holly Ganz, Ph.D.

Holly Ganz studies the microbiome of cats at the UC Davis Genome Center. She studies co-evolution of microbes and cats with an emphasis on cheetahs, lions, leopards, pumas, domestic cats and European wild cats. Previously she studied microbes in dogs’ mouths, mosquitoes and plants and she co-discovered a giant virus in a dead zebra. In addition to conducting research, she dotes on her two shelter dogs and provides foster care for cats and kittens for a local animal shelter.

Jonathan Eisen, Ph.D.

Jonathan Eisen studies communities of microbes at UC Davis and how they provide new functions – to each other or to a host. His study systems have included boiling acid pools, surface ocean waters, agents of many diseases, and the microbial ecosystems in and on plants and animals. In addition to his research, he is a vocal advocate for “open science.” He is also an active and award-winning blogger and science communicator.

Jack Gilbert, Ph.D.

Jack Gilbert uses molecular analysis and sequencing tools to test fundamental hypotheses in microbial ecology at Argonne National Laboratory and the University of Chicago. He is currently working on generating observational and mechanistic models of microbial communities in natural, urban, built and human ecosystems. Jack believes that cats provide an excellent model system for exploring disease and the microbiome, and hold great potential for developing translation therapies for those conditions. Among other projects, he leads the Earth Microbiome Project, Home Microbiome Project, Hospital Microbiome Project, and co-founded the American Gut Project.

Jennifer Gardy, Ph.D.

Jennifer Gardy studies bacterial genomes at the BC Centre for Disease Control, where she helped invent a technique for tracking outbreaks of infectious disease using genome sequencing. When not doing science, she hosts science television shows, including episodes of CBC Television’s long-running documentary The Nature of Things and Discovery Channel Canada’s nightly science newsmagazine Daily Planet. When not doing science or science television, she can be found at home in Vancouver, playing with her fat orange cat Danny and her Australian Shepherd Gypsy, both of whom likely have gut microbiomes dominated by bacteria that thrive on pet treats.
